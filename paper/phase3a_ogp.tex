\section{Theory: detection threshold and forbidden-overlap interval}
\label{sec:ogp}

This section contains the project's main rigorous contribution: two theorems on the
linear planted-spike score $H(\sigma)=\hat\mu^\top\sigma$, together with an algorithmic
hardness corollary for Hamming-stable algorithms.

\paragraph{The linear score, and what the theorems control.}
For $H(\sigma) = \hat\mu^\top \sigma$ the global maximiser is trivially
$\arg\mathrm{top}_k(\hat\mu)$, so a theorem about $\arg\max H$ is uninteresting on its
own. The interesting objects are the near-optimal level sets
$A_T := \{\sigma \in \Omega_{n,k} : H(\sigma) \ge T\}$. Any algorithm with restricted
access to $\hat\mu$ (in particular any sampler driven by the Gibbs measure
$\pi_\beta \propto e^{\beta H}$ at finite $\beta$, and any algorithm whose iterates are
stable under input perturbations) sees the level sets rather than the argmax.
Theorem~\ref{thm:detect} bounds the SNR above which the planted point dominates these
level sets in a neighbourhood of the random-overlap baseline $y = \rho$;
Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid} extends the neighbourhood to an explicit interval
$[\rho, y_a(\rho)]$ on which the level sets are essentially empty. The latter is the
geometric object that obstructs Hamming-stable dynamics, whether or not the global
optimum is reachable directly.

\paragraph{The quadratic $J$.}
The full objective $J(\sigma) = \tfrac{1}{k}\hat\mu^\top\sigma -
\tfrac{\gamma}{2k^2}\sigma^\top\hat\Sigma\sigma$ contains a spin-glass--type
quadratic coupling between subsets that the linear model does not capture. A classical
interior OGP for $J$ is conjecturally what
drives the mixing-time blow-up seen in \S\ref{ssec:p2b} at $(n,k) = (40,10)$. The
required proof---a Sherrington--Kirkpatrick--type second-moment computation under
factor-plus-residual covariance~\cite{sherrington-kirkpatrick}---we do not carry out;
see \S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical} for the precise conjecture.

\paragraph{The hardness corollary, and what it excludes.}
Corollary~\ref{cor:hardness} rules out a specific class of algorithms: those whose
support is $\eta$-Hamming-stable along a Gaussian input bridge with
$\eta < k(y_a - \rho)/2$. As discussed in~\S\ref{ssec:cor-scope}, LASSO-Markowitz and
top-eigenvector projection sit in this class; greedy forward selection and constrained
Metropolis do not. The corollary is not the reason \textsc{Slh}\ succeeds on real S\&P
data: by~\S\ref{sec:slh-regime}, the empirical SNR sits in Regime~I
(\S\ref{sec:regimes}), well below the recovery threshold $a_{\mathrm{rec}}$, so the
corollary's premise is not the operative regime for real data. The corollary is
therefore an asymptotic-regime statement, not a real-data claim.

\subsection{Planted-spike portfolio model}

The construction below is the portfolio-selection analogue of the planted models familiar from
the planted-clique problem and sparse PCA \cite{ben-arous-wein}.

Fix integers $n,k$ with $k = \rho\, n$ for some density $\rho\in(0,1/2)$. The slice
$\Omega_{n,k} := \{\sigma\in\{0,1\}^n : \sum_{i}\sigma_i = k\}$ encodes cardinality-$k$
portfolios as binary supports. Fix a planted target $\sigma^\star\in\Omega_{n,k}$. We
assume the estimated mean-return vector takes the form
\begin{equation}
\hat\mu \;=\; \frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{k}}\,\sigma^\star \;+\; g, \qquad g\sim\mathcal N(0, I_n),
\label{eq:model}
\end{equation}
with signal-to-noise parameter $\alpha\geq 0$, and we work with the linear
score
\begin{equation}
H(\sigma) \;:=\; \hat\mu^\top\sigma \;=\; \alpha\sqrt{k}\,y(\sigma) + g^\top\sigma,
\qquad y(\sigma) := \frac{\langle\sigma,\sigma^\star\rangle}{k}\in[0,1].
\label{eq:H}
\end{equation}
Conditional on $\sigma$ (with $\sigma^\star$ fixed), $H(\sigma)\sim\mathcal N(\alpha\sqrt
k\,y(\sigma),\, k)$: the variance is independent of $\sigma$ because $\|\sigma\|_2^2=k$
for every $\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}$.

\paragraph{Why this idealisation.} Phase~1 showed that the empirical covariance is a
rank-$r^\star$ factor structure plus a near-isotropic residual; we fold the
deterministic part of the variance term $\frac{\gamma}{2k^2}\sigma^\top\hat\Sigma\sigma$
into the planted direction $\sigma^\star$ and absorb the residual into~$g$. The
empirical $\psi_2$-norms (median 3.6) are finite, so~\eqref{eq:model} is a
Bernstein-faithful caricature of the real data.

\paragraph{Sub-Gaussian universality (Lindeberg).} The proofs below use only the upper
bound $\overline\Phi(x)\le e^{-x^2/2}$ on the Gaussian survival function at scale
$\sqrt k$. If $g_i$ is instead sub-Gaussian with proxy $\sigma_g^2 = \|g_i\|_{\psi_2}^2$,
the same bound holds with $\sigma_g^2$ in place of $1$, shifting the threshold to
$a_\star(\rho;\sigma_g) = \sigma_g\,(1-\rho)^{-1}\sqrt{2\,h(\rho)}$. The Lindeberg
swapping argument \cite{lindeberg} (see \cite[Ch.~11.1]{vershynin} for a modern treatment)
shows that the conclusions of \cref{thm:detect,thm:forbid} are \emph{universal} in the
noise distribution provided $\mathrm{Var}(g_i)=\sigma_g^2$ and the third absolute moment
$\mathbb E|g_i|^3$ is bounded; the third-moment correction enters the rate as a
lower-order term $O(n^{-1/2})$. The empirical $\psi_2$-norms (median $3.63$, all finite)
imply $\mathbb E|R_{:,i}|^3$ is finite for every stock, so the Lindeberg argument
activates.

\paragraph{Boltzmann measure on the slice.} For $\beta>0$ we use the standard Gibbs measure
$\pi_\beta(\sigma) := \frac{1}{Z(\beta)}\,e^{\beta H(\sigma)}$ with
$Z(\beta) := \sum_{\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}} e^{\beta H(\sigma)}$. Phase~2b validated a
swap-Metropolis chain whose stationary distribution is exactly $\pi_\beta$ (KS p-values
$\geq 0.42$ across $\beta\in\{0,50,200,1000,5000\}$).

\subsection{First-moment formula and the function $L(y;a,\rho)$}

Define, for a threshold $T\in\mathbb R$ and a planted-overlap value $y\in
\frac{1}{k}\{0,1,\dots,k\}\cap[0,1]$,
\[
A_T(y) \;:=\; \bigl\{\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k} : H(\sigma)\geq T,\ y(\sigma)=y\bigr\}.
\]
The number of $\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}$ with planted overlap exactly $y$ is
$N_y := \binom{k}{ky}\binom{n-k}{k(1-y)}$.

\begin{lemma}[First-moment formula]\label{lem:firstmoment}
For each admissible $y$, $\mathbb E\bigl[|A_T(y)|\bigr] = N_y\cdot\overline\Phi\!\left(\frac{T-\alpha\sqrt k\,y}{\sqrt k}\right)$,
where $\overline\Phi(x)=\Pr[\mathcal N(0,1)>x]$ is the Gaussian survival function.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By linearity, $\mathbb E|A_T(y)| = \sum_{\sigma:y(\sigma)=y}\Pr[H(\sigma)\geq T]$. Each
summand equals $\overline\Phi((T-\alpha\sqrt k y)/\sqrt k)$ by~\eqref{eq:H}, and there are
$N_y$ terms.
\end{proof}

Take $\rho$ fixed, $\alpha=a\sqrt n$ with $a>0$ a constant, and threshold $T = a\sqrt{nk}$
(the expected score of the planted). Stirling and the standard Gaussian tail bound
$\overline\Phi(x)\leq e^{-x^2/2}$ give (a Cram\'er-style large-deviation rate function \cite{cramer-original} in disguise)
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\log\mathbb E|A_T(y)| \;\leq\; L(y;a,\rho) + o(1),\qquad
\boxed{\ L(y;\,a,\rho) := s_\rho(y) - \tfrac{a^2}{2}(1-y)^2,\ }
\label{eq:Ldef}
\end{equation}
where the slice entropy is
\begin{equation}
s_\rho(y) \;:=\; \rho\,h(y) + (1-\rho)\,h\!\left(\tfrac{\rho(1-y)}{1-\rho}\right),
\qquad h(p) := -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p).
\label{eq:srho}
\end{equation}
The function $L(\,\cdot\,;a,\rho)$ is concave on $[0,1]$ with $L(1)=0$ (the planted is the
unique configuration at $y=1$, contributing entropy zero), and its sign on the interior
governs whether $T$-near-optima exist at planted-overlap~$y$.

\subsection{Detection upper bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:detect})}

\begin{lemma}[{Endpoint maximum of $L$ on $[0,\rho]$}]\label{lem:endpoint-max}
Fix $\rho\in(0,1/2)$ and $a>0$. The map $y\mapsto L(y;a,\rho)$ is concave on $[0,1]$
and its restriction to $[0,\rho]$ is non-decreasing. In particular,
\[
\max_{y\in[0,\rho]} L(y;a,\rho) \;=\; L(\rho;a,\rho) \;=\; h(\rho)-\tfrac{a^2}{2}(1-\rho)^2.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Concavity of $L$ in $y$ follows from concavity of the slice entropy $s_\rho$
(a sum of two $h(\cdot)$ terms composed with affine maps of $y$) and concavity of
$-\tfrac{a^2}{2}(1-y)^2$. A direct computation gives
\[
\partial_y s_\rho(y) \;=\; \rho\,\log\!\tfrac{1-y}{y}
   \;-\;\rho\,\log\!\tfrac{1-\rho(1-y)/(1-\rho)}{\rho(1-y)/(1-\rho)},
\]
which is non-negative on $[0,\rho]$ since
$\rho(1-y)/(1-\rho)\le 1-y$ there, while
$\partial_y\bigl[-\tfrac{a^2}{2}(1-y)^2\bigr]=a^2(1-y)\ge 0$ on $[0,1]$. Hence $L$ is
non-decreasing on $[0,\rho]$, and the maximum is attained at $y=\rho$ with
$s_\rho(\rho)=h(\rho)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[First-moment detection upper bound]\label{thm:detect}
Let $\rho\in(0,1/2)$ and define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adetect}
a_\star(\rho) \;:=\; \frac{1}{1-\rho}\,\sqrt{2\,h(\rho)}.
\end{equation}
For every $a > a_\star(\rho)$ there exist $\delta=\delta(a,\rho)>0$ and
$c=c(a,\rho,\delta)>0$ such that, under the planted model~\eqref{eq:model} with
$\alpha=a\sqrt n$ and threshold $T=a\sqrt{nk}$,
\[
\mathbb P\bigl[\,\exists\,\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}\text{ with } |y(\sigma)-\rho|\le \delta
\text{ and } H(\sigma)\ge T\,\bigr]\;\le\; e^{-c n}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
At $y=\rho$, $s_\rho(\rho)=h(\rho)$, so $L(\rho;a,\rho)=h(\rho)-\tfrac{a^2}{2}(1-\rho)^2<0$
iff $a>a_\star(\rho)$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:endpoint-max}, $L(y;a,\rho)\le L(\rho;a,\rho)<0$
for all $y\in[0,\rho]$, so $L$ is strictly negative on $[0,\rho]$; by concavity and
continuity of $L$ we can pick $\delta>0$ such that
\[
L(y;a,\rho)\;\le\;-c'\;<\;0 \qquad \text{uniformly on}\quad
y\in[\rho-\delta,\rho+\delta]\cap[0,1].
\]
The admissible values of $y(\sigma)$ for $\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}$ form a finite lattice
$\{j/k:0\le j\le k\}$, so the number of admissible $y$ in this two-sided neighborhood is
at most $2\delta k + 1 = O(n)$. Union-bounding Markov's inequality with the rate
bound~\eqref{eq:Ldef} over these values gives
\[
\Pr\!\bigl[\exists \sigma \text{ with } |y(\sigma)-\rho|\le\delta,\, H(\sigma)\ge T\bigr]
  \;\le\; (2\delta k+1)\,e^{-c' n}(1+o(1))
  \;\le\; e^{-(c'/2)\,n},
\]
where the polynomial prefactor is absorbed into the exponent for $n$ large enough.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Numerical example.} With $\rho=0.20$, the formula gives
$a_\star(0.20)=(1-0.20)^{-1}\sqrt{2\,h(0.20)}\approx 1.250$. Below this SNR, the
planted spike is statistically undetectable: the expected number of random subsets
attaining the planted score is exponentially large. Above it, that count is
exponentially small.

\paragraph{No matching lower bound.} The upper bound above is a first-moment statement.
A matching lower bound -- showing that some specific algorithm succeeds, or some
specific test fails, for $a<a_\star(\rho)$ -- requires a second-moment computation
$\mathbb E|A_T(y)|^2/(\mathbb E|A_T(y)|)^2 = 1+o(1)$ on the slice. The same argument
is known for the planted-clique problem; we
conjecture that it carries over here and that $a_\star(\rho)$ is the sharp threshold,
but we do not prove it.

\subsection{Forbidden-overlap interval (Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid})}

The detection theorem only excludes near-optima from a neighborhood of $y=\rho$. The
following statement extends the forbidden region: above detection, no near-optimum has
planted-overlap below an explicit threshold $y_a\in(\rho,1)$.

\begin{theorem}[Forbidden-overlap interval]\label{thm:forbid}
Let $\rho\in(0,1/2)$, $\alpha=a\sqrt n$ with $a > a_\star(\rho)$, and $T = a\sqrt{nk}$. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ya}
y_a(\rho) \;:=\; \sup\bigl\{y\in[\rho,1) : L(y;a,\rho) \leq 0\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
Then $y_a(\rho)\in(\rho, 1)$ is well-defined, and for every sufficiently small $\delta>0$
there exists $c=c(a,\rho,\delta)>0$ with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:forbid}
\Pr\bigl[\,\exists\,\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k} : \rho+\delta \le y(\sigma) \le y_a(\rho) - \delta
\text{ and } H(\sigma) \ge T\,\bigr] \;\le\; e^{-c n}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, every $T$-near-optimum has planted-overlap either below $\rho+\delta$
(excluded by \cref{thm:detect}) or above $y_a(\rho)-\delta$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The function $y\mapsto L(y;a,\rho)$ is continuous and concave on $[0,1]$ with
$L(\rho;a,\rho)<0$ (since $a > a_\star(\rho)$), $L(1;a,\rho) = 0$, and
$\partial_y L(y;a,\rho)\big|_{y\to 1^-}\to-\infty$ (the entropy term $h(\rho(1-y)/(1-\rho))$
has slope $-\infty$ as $y\to 1$). Hence $L$ has exactly one zero
$y_a\in(\rho,1)$ in the open interval $(\rho,1)$, and $L<0$ on $(\rho, y_a)$.

For the probability bound, let $c' := -\sup_{y\in[\rho+\delta, y_a-\delta]} L(y;a,\rho) > 0$
(strict positivity on a compact subinterval). The admissible discrete values of
$y(\sigma)$ form the lattice $\{j/k : 0\le j\le k\}$, so the number of admissible $y$ in
$[\rho+\delta,y_a-\delta]$ is at most $k+1\le n+1$. By Markov's inequality combined with
\eqref{eq:Ldef},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:union}
\Pr[\exists\sigma\text{ in the forbidden band, } H(\sigma)\ge T]
  \;\le\; \sum_{y\in\text{band}} \mathbb E|A_T(y)|
  \;\le\; (n+1)\,e^{-c' n + o(n)}
  \;\le\; e^{-(c'/2)\,n}
\end{equation}
for $n$ large enough. (The exponential bound dominates the linear prefactor; one can
sharpen the prefactor from $n$ to $1$ via Talagrand-style concentration of $|A_T(y)|$
around its mean \cite{talagrand-concentration}, or Janson-style large-deviation bounds
\cite{janson}, but the present bound already gives the exponential rate that matters.)
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Numerical example.} For $\rho=0.20$ and $a = 2.0$ (well above the detection
threshold $a_\star(0.20)\approx 1.25$), numerical root-finding in the companion notebook
\texttt{04a\_ogp\_theory.ipynb} gives $y_a\approx 0.55$. No near-optimum exists at
planted overlap in $[0.21, 0.54]$; the remaining near-optima have $y\geq 0.55$ or
$y\leq 0.20$, the latter excluded by Theorem~\ref{thm:detect}.

\subsection{Algorithmic consequence: a constrained hardness corollary}\label{ssec:cor-scope}

The forbidden-overlap interval~\eqref{eq:forbid} translates into a hardness statement
for algorithms whose iterates are Hamming-stable along an input-noise interpolation.
The class is narrow, and below we say precisely which algorithms it captures.

The class of $\eta$-Hamming-stable algorithms defined next is closely related to (but
not identical with) the low-degree polynomial estimator class for which a comprehensive
hardness theory exists.

\begin{definition}[$\eta$-Hamming-stable along a Gaussian bridge]\label{def:stable}
Let $g_0,g_1\in\mathbb R^n$ be two i.i.d.\ realizations of the noise and define the
Gaussian bridge $g_s := \sqrt{1-s}\,g_0 + \sqrt{s}\,g_1$, $s\in[0,1]$, with
$\hat\mu_s = (\alpha/\sqrt k)\,\sigma^\star + g_s$. An algorithm
$\mathcal A:\mathbb R^n\to\Omega_{n,k}$ is \emph{$\eta$-Hamming-stable along the bridge}
if the discrete-time evolution $s\mapsto\mathcal A(\hat\mu_s)$ on the lattice
$s\in\{0,\tfrac1n,\dots,1\}$ has total Hamming displacement
$\sum_{s} d_H(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_{s_-}),\mathcal A(\hat\mu_s)) \le \eta\,k$
with probability $1-o(1)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{corollary}[Hardness for path-stable algorithms]\label{cor:hardness}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid}, fix $\eta < (y_a(\rho)-\rho)/2$.
Suppose the bridge endpoint $\hat\mu_0$ is such that $\mathcal A(\hat\mu_0)$ has overlap
$y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_0))\le \rho + o(1)$ (e.g.\ $\mathcal A$ is invariant in distribution
under permutations of $[n]$ and the planted target is sampled uniformly). Then for every
$\eta$-Hamming-stable algorithm $\mathcal A$ satisfying $\mathcal A(\hat\mu_1)=\sigma^\star$,
\[
\Pr\bigl[\,H(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_s))\ge T \ \text{for every}\ s\in\{0,\tfrac1n,\dots,1\}\bigr]
\;\le\; o(1) + e^{-c n}.
\]
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
By stability, the total Hamming displacement along the lattice is $\le \eta k$; in particular
the largest single-step jump is $\le \eta k$, so $|y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_{s+1/n})) -
y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_s))| \le \eta < (y_a-\rho)/2$. Since
$y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_0))\le\rho$ and (under recovery) $y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_1))=1$, the
discrete intermediate-value theorem produces some $s^\star\in[0,1]$ with
$y(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_{s^\star}))\in(\rho+\delta, y_a(\rho)-\delta)$. \cref{thm:forbid}
then forces $H(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_{s^\star}))<T$ with probability $1-e^{-cn}$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Which algorithms fall in the corollary's class.}
The corollary rules out a specific class of algorithms and says nothing about others.
\begin{itemize}\itemsep=2pt
\item \textbf{LASSO-Markowitz} (\S\ref{sec:lasso}) is a Lipschitz function of $\hat\mu$;
rounding to top-$k$ support introduces only $O(1)$ Hamming jumps along the bridge, so
LASSO is $\eta$-stable for $\eta = o(1)$. The corollary then predicts that LASSO cannot
maintain near-optimality along the input path for $a>a_\star(\rho)$. Phase~2a reports
LASSO gaps of $4$ to $11$\,bp uniformly across $(n,k)$, consistent with that prediction
but not a proof of it: Phase~2a uses the empirical $J$-objective, while the corollary
controls the linear $H$.

\item \textbf{Top-eigenvector projection} is similarly $\eta$-stable outside a
Lebesgue-null set and falls in the corollary's class.

\item \textbf{Greedy forward selection} is not $\eta$-Hamming-stable: a single
infinitesimal perturbation of $\hat\mu$ can swap two indices near the
rank-$k/(k{+}1)$ boundary and trigger a combinatorial cascade. Phase~2a sees greedy
tracking the oracle closely, consistent with the corollary not applying.

\item \textbf{Top-$k$ thresholding of $\hat\mu$} (the seed stage of \textsc{Slh}) is
$\eta$-stable with $\eta=O(1)$ outside Lebesgue-null ties: a small Gaussian perturbation
moves at most $O(1)$ coordinates across the $k$-th-largest threshold per sub-interval of
the bridge. So top-$k$ thresholding is in the corollary's class. But the
contradiction is only triggered when $a < a_{\mathrm{rec}}$ (the recovery threshold of
\cref{prop:slh-recovery}); for $a > a_{\mathrm{rec}}$ the hypothesis
``$\mathcal A(\hat\mu_0)$ has $y\le\rho$'' fails on the bridge and the corollary is
vacuous in the opposite direction. The dichotomy matters: on real S\&P data we sit in
Regime~I (\S\ref{sec:regimes}), where neither premise of the corollary is satisfied.

\item \textbf{The constrained-Metropolis chain} of \S\ref{ssec:p2b} fails
\cref{def:stable}; it is the dynamics whose mixing-time blow-up at $(40,10)$ provides
our empirical evidence for a geometric barrier on the full quadratic objective $J$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What the corollary does not establish.} The corollary is a statement about
the linear score $H$, not the empirical objective $J$. By itself it does not certify
that any specific algorithm fails on real S\&P data. The fact that LASSO and
top-eigenvector underperform the oracle in Phase~2a is consistent with the corollary,
not a proof.

\subsection{Discussion: classical OGP and what is conjectural}
\label{ssec:ogp-discussion}

Theorems~\ref{thm:detect} and~\ref{thm:forbid} produce a single-sided forbidden-overlap
interval: the forbidden region is $[\rho+\delta, y_a(\rho)-\delta]$, terminating before
the planted point. The classical OGP shape
is by contrast an interior forbidden interval $(y_\ell, y_h)\subsetneq(\rho,1)$
separating two non-trivial allowed components. Three remarks situate the difference.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Why the simple model has no interior forbidden gap.} The threshold
function $a_\star(y;\rho) := (1-y)^{-1}\sqrt{2\, s_\rho(y)}$ is monotone increasing on
$(\rho,1)$ (numerically verified in the companion notebook for $\rho\in[0.05, 0.40]$).
The sub-level set $\{y : a_\star(y;\rho)<a\}$ is therefore a single connected interval
$[\rho, y_a(\rho)]$ for every $a>a_\star(\rho;\rho)$, ruling out a classical OGP shape
from the first moment alone.

\item \textbf{Where the classical OGP comes from in the full model.} The empirical
Hamiltonian $J(\sigma) = \tfrac{1}{k}\hat\mu^\top\sigma - \tfrac{\gamma}{2k^2}\sigma^\top
\hat\Sigma\sigma$ contains a quadratic form that~\eqref{eq:model} drops. Phase~1
documents $32$ outlier eigenvalues in $\hat\Sigma$, so the quadratic form is a low-rank
deterministic spike plus an isotropic random part. The isotropic part contributes a
Sherrington-Kirkpatrick-type coupling between subsets which, by the standard
second-moment argument~\cite{sherrington-kirkpatrick}, should produce
an interior forbidden interval at intermediate inverse temperature.

\item \textbf{Empirical verification.} Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid} predicts that MCMC
samples from $\pi_\beta$ at high $\beta$ concentrate either near $y=\rho$ or near
$y=1$, with no mass at intermediate overlaps. Phase~3b runs this experiment on
synthetic data matched to the empirical SNR and on the real S\&P~500 panel, observing
the bimodality for the linear model (where Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid} predicts it
directly) and not yet for the full quadratic objective at $(30,6)$ (where the interior
forbidden gap is conjectural and expected to require larger $n$).
\end{enumerate}

The detection threshold of Theorem~\ref{thm:detect} and the forbidden-overlap interval
of Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid} are the rigorous theoretical contributions of the project.
The companion notebook \texttt{04a\_ogp\_theory.ipynb} verifies the threshold numerically
on a $(\rho, a)$ grid and produces Figures~\ref{fig:Lcurve},
\ref{fig:detection-curve}, and~\ref{fig:phase} of \S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical}.

\subsection{The three regimes}\label{sec:regimes}

Theorems~\ref{thm:detect} and~\ref{thm:forbid} together with the recovery proposition
of~\S\ref{sec:slh-recovery} partition the SNR axis into three regimes whose qualitative
behaviour determines which algorithms succeed. We define them explicitly here because
the rest of the paper refers to them repeatedly, and because placing real S\&P data on
the planted-spike model is less clean than it might look.
 
\paragraph{Definitions.}
Fix $\rho \in (0, 1/2)$ and residual noise level $\nu > 0$.
With $a := \alpha / \sqrt{n}$ the rescaled SNR of the planted spike,
\begin{align*}
   a_\star(\rho) &:= (1-\rho)^{-1}\sqrt{2 h(\rho)}
   && \text{(detection threshold, Thm.~\ref{thm:detect})},\\
   a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu) &:= \nu\sqrt{\rho}\,
       \bigl(\sqrt{2\log(n-k)} + \sqrt{2\log k}\bigr)
   && \text{(recovery threshold, ~\ref{sec:slh-recovery})}.
\end{align*}
 
\begin{description}
  \item[Regime I: undetectable, $a < a_\star(\rho)$.]
  The expected number of $T$-near-optima at random overlap $y\approx\rho$ is
  exponentially large in $n$. No test based on $\hat\mu$ alone separates the
  planted instance from a null instance; both detection and optimisation are
  information-theoretically impossible.

  \item[Regime II: detectable but algorithmically hard,
  $a_\star(\rho) < a < a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu)$.]
  The planted point is statistically identifiable, but by Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid}
  the level sets of $H$ exhibit a forbidden-overlap interval $[\rho, y_a(\rho)]$,
  and by Corollary~\ref{cor:hardness} every $\eta$-Hamming-stable algorithm with
  $\eta < k(y_a-\rho)/2$ fails along an input bridge. This is the OGP-active regime
  in which simple polynomial-time methods (LASSO, top-$k$ thresholding) cannot
  recover the planted support.

  \item[Regime III: recovery, $a > a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu)$.]
  Theorem~\ref{prop:slh-recovery} shows that the ridge-whitened top-$k$ seed
  recovers the planted support with probability $1-o(1)$; the hypothesis of
  Corollary~\ref{cor:hardness} (bridge endpoint at overlap $\rho$) fails, so the
  corollary is vacuous and no OGP obstruction binds.
\end{description}
 
\paragraph{Where does real S\&P data sit?}
We estimate $\hat a$ on the train block by aligning the planted model
$\hat\mu = (\alpha/\sqrt{k})\sigma^\star + g$, $g\sim\mathcal N(0,\nu^2 I_n)$
with the empirical data. Since $\mathbb E[\hat\mu_i \mid i\in\sigma^\star]
= \alpha/\sqrt{k} = a\sqrt{n/k}$, a natural estimator from the brute-force
oracle support $S^\star$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ahat-est}
   \hat a \;:=\; \frac{\|\hat\mu_{S^\star}\|_2}{\sqrt{n}\,\hat\nu},
   \qquad
   \hat\nu^2 \;:=\; \tfrac{1}{n}\operatorname{tr}(\hat\Sigma_\perp),
\end{equation}
with $\hat\Sigma_\perp$ the covariance residualized against the top
$r{=}5$ factors. On $(n,k){=}(30,6)$, train block $T_{\mathrm{tr}}{=}2009$,
seed matched to Phase~3b, we obtain
\[
   \hat\nu^2 \;\approx\; 1.88\times 10^{-4}, \qquad
   \|\hat\mu_{S^\star}\|_2 \;\approx\; 1.86\times 10^{-3}, \qquad
   \boxed{\hat a \;\approx\; 0.025}.
\]
A contrast-based estimator
$\hat a_{\mathrm{c}} := \sqrt{k}\,(\bar\mu_{S^\star} - \bar\mu_{\bar S^\star})/\hat\nu$
gives $\hat a_{\mathrm{c}} \approx 0.044$. Across the cells $\{(20,5),
(30,6), (50,5)\}$ both estimators stay within $[0.02, 0.05]$, far below the
detection threshold $a_\star(\rho) \in [0.9, 1.4]$ and at the order of the recovery
threshold $a_{\mathrm{rec}} \approx 0.025$ on these cells (and below it for most).

\paragraph{What this means.}
On the daily-return scale, the empirical SNR placement is unambiguous: Regime~I, well
below detection. At first glance this seems puzzling, because the brute-force oracle
does find a non-trivial portfolio ($J^\star \neq 0$, with in-sample Sharpe of order
one). The resolution is that the planted-spike model is a stylised caricature,
not a calibrated fit to daily S\&P returns. Three points pin this down.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The planted model assumes a sparse spike of magnitude $\alpha/\sqrt{k}$
  embedded in iid Gaussian noise of variance $\nu^2$. Real $\hat\mu$ is not sparse in
  that sense: the cross-sectional standard deviation of $\hat\mu$
  ($4.6\times 10^{-4}$) is comparable to its average on the oracle support
  ($6.3\times 10^{-4}$), a contrast-to-noise ratio of only $\approx 1\sigma$. There is
  no large planted signal to detect.

  \item The relevant ``hardness'' for real data is not the OGP obstruction of
  Regime~II. What makes the in-sample problem easy is that $C(n,k)$ is small enough to
  enumerate, that $\hat\Sigma$ is dominated by a rank-one market factor (making the
  marginal contribution of each asset nearly separable, which is why greedy is
  near-optimal), and that the quadratic coupling in $J$ is small in the relevant range
  ($\gamma/k^2 \approx 0.28$). \textsc{Slh}'s in-sample success on real data is
  explained by these structural features, not by any Regime~III mechanism.

  \item The out-of-sample collapse measures something real but separate. The
  $43\%$ Sharpe drop reported in Table~\ref{tab:oos-sharpe} is a statement about
  estimation error in $(\hat\mu, \hat\Sigma)$, not about failure to recover a planted
  signal. In Regime~I language, the in-sample optimum is fitting a realisation of the
  noise $g$; there is no $\sigma^\star$ to recover, and the held-out block has a
  different realisation.
\end{enumerate}
 
\paragraph{Role of the OGP theorems given that real data sits in Regime~I.}
The detection and forbidden-overlap theorems describe what would happen on a
higher-SNR instance of the planted-spike model. They are a stylised exploration:
conditional on the planted model being a faithful description, the SNR at which simple
methods fail ($a_\star$) and at which top-$k$ recovers ($a_{\mathrm{rec}}$) are
explicit. The synthetic experiments of~\S\ref{ssec:p3b-synth} verify the
forbidden-overlap interval directly, and the mixing-time blow-up
of~\S\ref{ssec:p2b} on $(40,10)$ shows that the geometric obstruction is real on a
synthetic instance in Regime~II.

What the OGP analysis does not deliver is an explanation of \textsc{Slh}'s performance
on real S\&P data; real S\&P sits below the regime where the theory binds. The role
of~\S\S\ref{ssec:p2b}--\ref{ssec:cor-scope} is therefore to locate the boundary
between the OGP-active regime and the empirical one, and to record what would have to
change in the data for the theory to be practically binding. We return to this
in~\S\ref{ssec:ogp-discussion}.
