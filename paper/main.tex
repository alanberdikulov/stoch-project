\begin{abstract}
We study cardinality-constrained portfolio selection on $n=468$ S\&P~500 constituents
over a 10-year window ($T=2{,}512$ trading days). The empirical version of the problem
has two faces. In-sample, it is computationally easy: a deterministic spectral seed
followed by a 1-swap polish (\textsc{Slh}) matches the brute-force oracle on every
$(n,k)$ cell within reach of enumeration, at roughly $0.1$\,ms per call. Out-of-sample,
the same oracle's annualised Sharpe collapses by $30$--$50\%$, and so do the Sharpes of
greedy, simulated annealing, and \textsc{Slh}, since all three recover the same support.
What limits performance on held-out data is the noise in $\hat\mu$ and $\hat\Sigma$, not
the difficulty of optimising over them.

For the planted linear score $H(\sigma)=\hat\mu^\top\sigma$ we prove a first-moment
detection upper bound $a_\star(\rho)= (1-\rho)^{-1}\sqrt{2 h(\rho)}$
(\cref{thm:detect}) and a single-sided forbidden-overlap interval
(\cref{thm:forbid}), both verified by constrained Metropolis. For the full quadratic
objective $J(\sigma)=\tfrac{1}{k}\hat\mu^\top\sigma-\tfrac{\gamma}{2k^2}\sigma^\top
\hat\Sigma\sigma$, a sharp mixing-time blow-up of constrained Metropolis between
$(n,k)=(20,5)$ and $(40,10)$ supplies empirical evidence for a classical interior OGP;
a rigorous proof remains open.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The problem.} Given $n$ stocks with daily returns $R\in\mathbb R^{T\times n}$,
sample mean $\hat\mu = \tfrac1T R^\top\mathbf 1$ and sample covariance $\hat\Sigma$, fix a
target portfolio size $k$ and risk-aversion $\gamma>0$. Following Markowitz \cite{markowitz},
the cardinality-constrained mean-variance problem is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:problem-intro}
  \sigma^\star \;=\; \arg\max_{\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}} J(\sigma),
  \qquad J(\sigma) := \frac{1}{k}\,\hat\mu^\top\sigma - \frac{\gamma}{2k^2}\sigma^\top\hat\Sigma\sigma,
\end{equation}
with $\Omega_{n,k}=\{\sigma\in\{0,1\}^n:\sum_i\sigma_i=k\}$. The feasible set has size
$\binom{n}{k}$; brute force gives out past $\sim\!10^7$, e.g.\ $\binom{50}{10}\approx 10^{10}$.

\paragraph{Two questions.} The paper organises itself around two questions:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep=2pt
\item \textbf{In-sample}: how hard is~\eqref{eq:problem-intro} on a given empirical
$(\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma)$? A discrete optimisation problem on a specific noisy input.
\item \textbf{Out-of-sample}: does solving (1) buy a portfolio that performs on
held-out data? A generalisation problem.
\end{enumerate}
The two answers go in opposite directions. On real S\&P~500 data
the in-sample problem is solved deterministically in $0.1$\,ms per call,
matching the brute-force oracle. Yet the oracle itself loses $30$--$50\%$ of its
training Sharpe out-of-sample. Whatever costs there are sit in the moments
$(\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma)$, not in the optimisation over them.

\paragraph{Why discrete probability?} The interaction between sample noise in
$(\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma)$ and the combinatorial structure of $\Omega_{n,k}$ is exactly what
the probabilistic-combinatorics toolkit was built for. Three ingredients enter:
concentration for $\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma$, which controls in-sample rankings; the spectrum
of $\hat\Sigma$ via random-matrix theory, which determines when a convex relaxation can
match the combinatorial optimum; and the Overlap Gap Property
(OGP), which gives a geometric theory of when
planted recovery is blocked for whole classes of algorithms.

\paragraph{What is proved, and what is conjectured.} We work in a planted-spike model
$\hat\mu = (\alpha/\sqrt k)\sigma^\star + g$ tailored to the factor-plus-residual
structure documented in~\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}. For the linear score
$H(\sigma)=\hat\mu^\top\sigma$ we prove a detection upper bound
(\cref{thm:detect}) and a single-sided forbidden-overlap interval (\cref{thm:forbid}).
On this linearised model the optimisation is trivially solved by top-$k$ thresholding
once $a>a_{\mathrm{rec}}$; the theorems are interesting not because they describe how
to optimise but because they pin down the information-theoretic boundary between random
and planted instances. For the quadratic objective $J$ in \eqref{eq:problem-intro}, the
classical interior-OGP shape remains conjectural here;
our empirical handle is the mixing time of constrained Metropolis, which blows up
sharply between $(n,k)=(20,5)$ and $(40,10)$ (\S\ref{ssec:p2b}).

\paragraph{Roadmap.}\S\ref{sec:diagnostics} characterises the empirical regime
(sub-Gaussian tails, factor-spike covariance, RIP failure). \S\ref{sec:oracle} sets up
the brute-force oracle, four polynomial-time baselines, and the constrained-Metropolis
sampler whose mixing-time blow-up provides the empirical OGP signal.
\S\ref{sec:ogp} proves the linear-model theorems and derives a hardness statement for
algorithms whose iterates are Hamming-stable along an input bridge.
\S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical} verifies the theorems on synthetic data and shows that the
analogous overlap distribution on real $(30,6)$ instances remains smooth.
\S\ref{sec:slh} introduces our hybrid algorithm \textsc{Slh}\ and gives an
exact-recovery proposition under factor-plus-residual covariance.
\S\ref{sec:conclusion} returns to the two questions opened above.
