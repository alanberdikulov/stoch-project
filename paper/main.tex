\begin{abstract}
We study cardinality-constrained portfolio selection on $n=468$ S\&P~500 constituents
over a 10-year window ($T=2{,}512$ trading days), addressing Project~7's question:
\emph{how hard is the problem from an empirical computational point of view?} Our answer
calibrates the difficulty to three regimes parameterised by a planted-spike SNR $a$.
Regime~I ($a < a_\star(\rho)$) is statistically undetectable. Regime~II
($a_\star(\rho)<a<a_{\mathrm{rec}}$) is information-theoretically possible but blocked for
all path-stable algorithms (LASSO, top-eigenvector, fixed-temperature MCMC) by an
Overlap Gap Property (OGP). Regime~III ($a>a_{\mathrm{rec}}$) is recoverable by simple
top-$k$ thresholding. The empirical S\&P~500 panel sits at the boundary of
Regimes~II and~III. We \emph{prove} a sharp first-moment detection threshold
$a_\star(\rho)=\sqrt{2h(\rho)}/(1-\rho)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:detect}) and a
forbidden-overlap interval (Theorem~\ref{thm:forbid}) for the planted-spike model, both
verified empirically via Metropolis sampling. We then design a deterministic spectral$+$local-improvement
algorithm \textsc{Slh}\ that (i) admits a Bonferroni recovery guarantee
(Proposition~\ref{prop:slh-recovery}) and (ii) circumvents the OGP barrier by virtue of
a discontinuous top-$k$ thresholding step that violates the path-stability hypothesis of
the hardness corollary. \textsc{Slh}\ matches the brute-force oracle on every cell of the
$(n,k)$ grid we test, while running $50\times$ faster than its closest non-deterministic
competitor.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and the project question}\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The problem.} Project~7 of MIT's discrete-probability course asks the
practical question: take real stock-exchange data, fix a portfolio size $k$, and consider
the problem of choosing $k$ stocks to maximise return subject to a risk constraint, asking
how hard this problem is empirically. We give the question a precise mathematical setting:
$n$ stocks with daily-return matrix $R\in\mathbb R^{T\times n}$, sample mean $\hat\mu$ and
sample covariance $\hat\Sigma$, risk-aversion $\gamma>0$, and the cardinality-constrained
mean-variance objective
\[
  J(\sigma) \;=\; \frac{1}{k}\,\hat\mu^\top\sigma \;-\; \frac{\gamma}{2k^2}\,\sigma^\top\hat\Sigma\sigma,
  \qquad \sigma\in\Omega_{n,k} := \{\sigma\in\{0,1\}^n:\textstyle\sum_i\sigma_i=k\}.
\]
The decision problem is to find $\sigma^\star = \arg\max_{\sigma\in\Omega_{n,k}} J(\sigma)$.
The space $\Omega_{n,k}$ has size $\binom{n}{k}$ and is too large for brute-force
enumeration past $\sim\!10^7$ (e.g., $\binom{50}{10}\approx 10^{10}$).

\paragraph{Why discrete probability?} The interplay between the empirical $(\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma)$,
a sample-noise object, and the combinatorial structure of $\Omega_{n,k}$ puts the problem
squarely in the territory of probabilistic combinatorics. Three conceptual pillars apply:
(i) \emph{concentration} of $\hat\mu, \hat\Sigma$ around their population values, which
controls how confident we can be in any sub-$k$ ranking;
(ii) the \emph{spectrum of $\hat\Sigma$} via random-matrix theory, which dictates whether
convex relaxations recover the combinatorial optimum; and (iii) the \emph{Overlap Gap Property},
which gives a geometric and statistical theory of when an instance is computationally hard
regardless of algorithmic effort.

\paragraph{Roadmap.}\S\ref{sec:diagnostics} characterises the empirical regime
(sub-Gaussian tails, factor-spike covariance, RIP failure). \S\ref{sec:oracle} establishes
the brute-force oracle and four polynomial-time baselines, plus the constrained-Metropolis
sampler whose mixing-time blow-up provides empirical evidence of an OGP barrier.
\S\ref{sec:ogp} proves the project's two main theorems on the planted-spike model and
derives the corresponding hardness corollary for path-stable algorithms.
\S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical} verifies the theorems on synthetic data and probes the
classical-OGP conjecture on real data. \S\ref{sec:slh} introduces our hybrid algorithm
\textsc{Slh}, proves a recovery proposition, and explains why the algorithm escapes the
hardness corollary. \S\ref{sec:conclusion} returns to the project question and gives a
direct answer in terms of the three regimes.
