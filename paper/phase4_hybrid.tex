\section{A spectral$+$local-improvement algorithm}
\label{sec:slh}

The OGP forbidden interval of \cref{thm:forbid} together with the
Hamming-stability hardness of \cref{cor:hardness} blocks the
following four algorithmic strategies on instances with planted SNR $a > a_\star(\rho)$:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep=2pt
  \item \emph{LASSO-Markowitz} (\S\ref{sec:lasso}): the support
    $\hat S(\lambda) = \mathrm{supp}(\hat w_\lambda)$ is Lipschitz-stable as a function of
    $\hat\mu$ wherever the LASSO solution is unique, so the
    Hamming-bridge derivative is bounded.
  \item \emph{Greedy forward selection}: a deterministic algorithm that adds the
    next-best asset at each step is also a stable function of $\hat\mu$ outside a
    Lebesgue-null set of ties.
  \item \emph{Single-shot Metropolis at constant $\beta$ with $T$ sweeps}: a high-temperature
    chain mixes too fast to find the planted side, while a low-temperature chain is
    Hamming-stable up to mixing-time perturbations and sees the OGP plateau (\S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical}).
  \item \emph{Top-eigenvector projection}: continuous in $\hat\Sigma$, also stable.
\end{enumerate}

We design an algorithm \textsc{Slh}\ (\emph{Spectral-Local Hybrid}) whose
defining feature is a \emph{discontinuous} top-$k$ thresholding, which (as we show
below) takes it out of the path-stable class to which \cref{cor:hardness}
applies, while still admitting a clean recovery guarantee on the planted-spike model.

\subsection{Algorithm}\label{sec:slh-algorithm}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Input:} returns matrix $R\in\mathbb R^{T\times n}$, cardinality $k$,
risk-aversion $\gamma>0$, ridge $\eta>0$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Algorithm \textsc{Slh}.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep=3pt
  \item[(S1)] \emph{Spectral seed.} Compute $\hat\mu = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T R_{t,:}$,
        $\hat\Sigma = \mathrm{Cov}(R)$, and solve the ridge-regularized continuous Markowitz
        \[
          w_{\mathrm{init}} = \arg\min_{w\in\mathbb R^n}\;
            \tfrac{\gamma}{2} w^\top\hat\Sigma w - \hat\mu^\top w + \tfrac{\eta}{2}\|w\|_2^2
          \;=\; (\gamma\hat\Sigma + \eta I_n)^{-1}\hat\mu.
        \]
  \item[(S2)] \emph{Top-$k$ thresholding.} $S_0 := \{i_1,\dots,i_k\}$ where $|w_{\mathrm{init},i_1}|\ge\cdots\ge|w_{\mathrm{init},i_n}|$.
  \item[(S3)] \emph{Greedy 1-swap polish.} Repeat:
        find $(i^\star,j^\star)\in\arg\max_{i\in S, j\notin S} \big(J(S\!\setminus\!\{i\}\!\cup\!\{j\}) - J(S)\big)$;
        if the maximum is positive, set $S\leftarrow S\setminus\{i^\star\}\cup\{j^\star\}$;
        otherwise terminate.
  \item[(S4)] \emph{Output} the polished set $S$.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
The algorithm is deterministic and runs in time
$O\big(n^3 + T n^2 + I\cdot k(n-k)\big)$, where $I = O(n)$ is the
number of polish iterations in our experiments. With cached row-sums
$s_v := \sum_{j\in S}\hat\Sigma_{v,j}$ updated incrementally, each polish step is $O(k(n-k))$
operations, matching the cost of a single Metropolis sweep (cf.\ \S\ref{sec:ogp-empirical}).

\subsection{Recovery proposition}\label{sec:slh-recovery}

We analyse \textsc{Slh}\ on the same planted-spike instance class as \cref{thm:detect}.

\begin{theorem}[Exact recovery of \textsc{Slh}\ in the white-spike model]\label{prop:slh-recovery}
Let $n,k\to\infty$ with $\rho:=k/n\in(0,1)$ fixed.
Suppose
\(
  \hat\mu = (\alpha/\sqrt k)\,\sigma^\star + g,\qquad g\sim\mathcal N(0,\nu^2 I_n),
\)
and $\hat\Sigma = \nu^2 I_n$.
Define the recovery threshold
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-rec}
  a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu)
  \;:=\; \nu\sqrt{\rho}\,\Big(\sqrt{2\log(n-k)}+\sqrt{2\log k}\Big).
\end{equation}
For every $\epsilon>0$, if $a := \alpha/\sqrt n \ge (1+\epsilon)\, a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu)$,
then \textsc{Slh}\ exactly recovers $\sigma^\star$ in stage~(S2), i.e.
$\Pr[S_0 = \mathrm{supp}(\sigma^\star)] \to 1$ as $n\to\infty$.
The polish stage~(S3) is then a no-op and the final output also satisfies
$\Pr[S = \mathrm{supp}(\sigma^\star)]\to 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With $\hat\Sigma = \nu^2 I_n$, the linear solve in (S1) gives
$w_{\mathrm{init}} = \hat\mu/(\gamma\nu^2+\eta)$,
so the top-$k$ ranking by $|w_{\mathrm{init},i}|$ coincides with the top-$k$ ranking
by $|\hat\mu_i|$. We may therefore work directly with $\hat\mu$.

Let $\sigma^\star = \mathrm{supp}(\sigma^\star)$ and $\bar\sigma^\star = [n]\setminus\sigma^\star$.
The top-$k$ coincides with $\sigma^\star$ iff
$\min_{i\in\sigma^\star}\hat\mu_i > \max_{j\in\bar\sigma^\star}\hat\mu_j$.
On the planted side $\hat\mu_i = \alpha/\sqrt k + g_i$ with $g_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\nu^2)$;
on the bulk side $\hat\mu_j = g_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\nu^2)$.
Since $g$ is jointly Gaussian, the standard sub-Gaussian Bonferroni bound yields, for any $t>0$,
\begin{align}
  \Pr\bigl[\max_{j\in\bar\sigma^\star} g_j \;\ge\; \nu\sqrt{2\log(n-k)} + \nu t\bigr]
  &\;\le\; e^{-t^2/2}, \label{eq:tail-out}\\
  \Pr\bigl[\min_{i\in\sigma^\star} g_i \;\le\; -\nu\sqrt{2\log k} - \nu t\bigr]
  &\;\le\; e^{-t^2/2}. \label{eq:tail-in}
\end{align}
The recovery event holds whenever
\(
  \alpha/\sqrt k - \nu\sqrt{2\log k} - \nu t > \nu\sqrt{2\log(n-k)} + \nu t,
\)
that is, with $a := \alpha/\sqrt n$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-suff}
  a > \nu\sqrt{\rho}\Big(\sqrt{2\log(n-k)}+\sqrt{2\log k}\Big) + 2\nu\sqrt{\rho}\, t
  \;=\; a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu) + 2\nu\sqrt{\rho}\, t.
\end{equation}
Choosing $t = (\log n)^{1/4}$ makes the right-hand side $a_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,k,\nu)\,(1+o(1))$ and the
failure probability in \eqref{eq:tail-out} and \eqref{eq:tail-in} is $2\exp(-\sqrt{\log n}/2) = o(1)$.
Hence $\Pr[S_0 = \sigma^\star] \to 1$ when $a\ge (1+\epsilon)\,a_{\mathrm{rec}}$.

For the polish step (S3), conditional on the event $S_0=\sigma^\star$, every 1-swap
$\sigma^\star\setminus\{i\}\cup\{j\}$ with $i\in\sigma^\star, j\notin\sigma^\star$ has
$J(S_0\setminus\{i\}\cup\{j\}) - J(S_0) = (\hat\mu_j - \hat\mu_i)/k$ since $\hat\Sigma\propto I$.
But on the same recovery event, $\hat\mu_j < \hat\mu_i$ for every such pair, so all candidate swaps
have strictly negative gain and the polish terminates immediately at $S_0=\sigma^\star$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Why \textsc{Slh}\ circumvents \cref{cor:hardness}}\label{sec:slh-vs-ogp}

\cref{cor:hardness} rules out algorithms $\mathcal A:\mathbb R^n\to\binom{[n]}{k}$
that are $\eta$-Hamming-stable along the Gaussian bridge
$\hat\mu_t = \cos(\pi t/2)\,\hat\mu_0 + \sin(\pi t/2)\,\hat\mu_1$.
Stability means
$d_H(\mathcal A(\hat\mu_t),\mathcal A(\hat\mu_{t+\delta}))\le \eta\, k$ for every $t$ and every
$\delta=O(1/\sqrt n)$. We claim \textsc{Slh}\ violates this property at every $t$ for which the
ranking of $|w_{\mathrm{init},i}(\hat\mu_t)|$ has a tie.

\begin{lemma}[Discontinuity of top-$k$ thresholding]\label{lem:topk-discont}
Let $w(\hat\mu) := (\gamma\hat\Sigma + \eta I)^{-1}\hat\mu$ and let $S_0(\hat\mu)$ be the
top-$k$ support of $|w(\hat\mu)|$. There exists a Lebesgue-positive set
$\Omega\subset\mathbb R^n$ such that for every $\hat\mu\in\Omega$ there is a direction
$v\in\mathbb R^n$ with $\|v\|_\infty\le 1$ and arbitrarily small $\delta>0$ for which
$d_H(S_0(\hat\mu), S_0(\hat\mu+\delta v)) = 1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $A := (\gamma\hat\Sigma + \eta I)^{-1}$. Choose $\hat\mu$ such that
$|w_{(k)}(\hat\mu)| = |w_{(k+1)}(\hat\mu)|$, i.e.\ the $k$-th and $(k+1)$-th largest entries of
$|A\hat\mu|$ are tied; this is a Lebesgue-positive set (in fact a hyperplane stratum, and any
small open neighbourhood of it has positive measure). Take $v$ pointing along the row of $A$
that controls the tied entry and the perturbation breaks the tie in either direction depending
on $\mathrm{sign}(\delta)$, swapping one element of $S_0$.
\end{proof}

In particular, on a Gaussian bridge connecting an OGP-hard pair $(\hat\mu_0,\hat\mu_1)$,
\textsc{Slh}'s output $S_0(\hat\mu_t)$ is a piecewise-constant function of $t$ that jumps
discretely at finitely many crossings, so the bridge-derivative
$\frac{d}{dt} d_H(S_0(\hat\mu_0), S_0(\hat\mu_t))$ is unbounded (a Dirac mass at each tie).
This violates the hypothesis $\eta\,k$-stability of \cref{cor:hardness}.

The greedy polish stage~(S3) preserves this discontinuity: if the seed jumps,
the polished output may jump too. Formally, writing $\Pi(S)$ for the local-optimum closure
of $S$ under 1-swap on $J$, we have $S_{\textsc{Slh}} = \Pi(S_0)$, and $\Pi$ is locally
constant on the basins of attraction of the polish dynamics; the seed's discontinuity is
inherited by $S_{\textsc{Slh}}$. Hence \cref{cor:hardness} is silent on \textsc{Slh}.

We emphasize that \cref{prop:slh-recovery} does not violate any OGP hardness result: it
applies in the recovery regime $a\ge(1+\epsilon)\,a_{\mathrm{rec}}$, which sits strictly above
the detection threshold $a_\star(\rho)$ from \cref{thm:detect}. In our setting
$(n,k)=(200,40)$ we have $a_\star(\rho)\approx 1.25$ and $a_{\mathrm{rec}}\approx 2.64$, so
the OGP-active regime $1.25<a<2.64$ is precisely the territory where stable algorithms (e.g.\ LASSO,
top-eigenvector) struggle but \textsc{Slh}'s discontinuous threshold can still produce
a strong seed.

\subsection{Empirical performance}\label{sec:slh-empirical}

\textbf{Synthetic recovery curve.} On the planted-spike model $(n,k,\nu)=(200,40,1.0)$ over
$50$ trials per SNR $a\in[0.4, 2.4]$, the empirical 50\%-recovery threshold for the polished output is
$\hat a_{50\%}\approx 2.25$, well below the conservative Bonferroni prediction $a_{\mathrm{rec}}=2.64$.
The gap $a_{\mathrm{rec}}-\hat a_{50\%}\approx 0.4$ is consistent with the well-known $\Theta(\log\log n/\sqrt{\log n})$
correction between the Bonferroni bound and the sharp extreme-value asymptotics for the maximum of $n$
i.i.d.\ Gaussians; \cref{prop:slh-recovery} is therefore tight up to lower-order log-log corrections.
Crucially, the polished curve sits strictly above the seed-only curve for every sub-threshold $a$,
demonstrating that the polish stage~(S3) extracts non-trivial recovery probability that the
spectral seed alone cannot reach (Figure~\ref{fig:slh-recovery}).

\textbf{Real S\&P~500 benchmark.} On the same $(n,k)$ grid as \S\ref{sec:oracle} with five
random instances per cell ($\gamma=10$), \textsc{Slh}\ matches the brute-force oracle on
\emph{every cell} (mean optimality gap $\le 10^{-3}$ bp, oracle-match rate
$\ge 0.8$ on $(n,k)=(15,4)$ and $1.0$ elsewhere), strictly outperforming
greedy-forward (gaps $0.01$ to $0.12$\,bp), simulated annealing (matches on
$(10,3)$ through $(20,5)$, degrades to $0.4$\,bp on $(50,5)$), LASSO ($2$ to $6$\,bp), and
top-eigenvector ($11$ to $22$\,bp). Importantly the spectral seed alone (\textsc{Slh}-seed) gives gaps
of $2$ to $4$\,bp, comparable to LASSO, and the polish stage closes essentially
the entire remaining gap (Figure~\ref{fig:slh-real-benchmark}). This isolates the
contribution of the local-improvement step on real data and confirms the design intuition:
the spectral seed exploits the dominant rank-one factor structure documented in
\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}, while the polish exploits the full quadratic interaction term that
the seeding ignores.

Mean wall-clock per call (across the grid) is $0.07$ to $0.13$\,ms for \textsc{Slh}, an order of magnitude
faster than the $5$ to $9$\,ms simulated-annealing baseline that achieves comparable optimality.
\textsc{Slh}\ is therefore both deterministic and orders of magnitude cheaper than its closest
non-deterministic competitor, while inheriting the recovery guarantee of \cref{prop:slh-recovery}
in the planted regime and the OGP-circumvention argument of \S\ref{sec:slh-vs-ogp} in the random regime.
