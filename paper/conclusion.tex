\section{Discussion: how hard is the problem?}\label{sec:conclusion}

The project asked how hard cardinality-constrained portfolio selection is empirically.
On real S\&P~500 data, the answers to the in-sample and out-of-sample versions of the
question move in opposite directions.

\paragraph{In-sample, the problem is computationally easy.}
A deterministic spectral seed plus a 1-swap polish (\textsc{Slh}) matches the
brute-force oracle on every cell of the $(n,k)$ grid within reach of enumeration, at
roughly $0.1$\,ms per call. Greedy forward selection stays within $0.05$ to
$0.12$\,bp of the oracle; simulated annealing matches it on small cells and degrades
gently on larger ones; \textsc{Slh}\ combines the strengths of both deterministically.
The combinatorial behaviour the OGP literature warns about (convex relaxations
stalling in a forbidden-overlap plateau) is not the empirical bottleneck. This is not
because the empirical SNR sits above the recovery threshold, as a quick reading of
the regime framework might suggest. Per Table~\ref{tab:regime-numbers}, real S\&P
data sits in Regime~I on the daily-return scale: the planted-spike model fits poorly,
and the OGP machinery is vacuous in both directions. What makes \textsc{Slh}\ work on
this dataset is structural -- rank-one factor dominance of $\hat\Sigma$, small
$C(n,k)$, weak quadratic coupling $\gamma/k^2$ -- rather than regime-driven
(\S\ref{sec:slh-regime}). The OGP theorems remain useful as a stylised exploration of
what would happen at higher SNR; the synthetic experiments
of~\S\ref{ssec:p3b-synth} and the mixing-time blow-up of~\S\ref{ssec:p2b} verify the
predicted behaviour where the theory binds.

\paragraph{Out-of-sample, the problem is statistically hard.} This is the main
practical finding of the project. Across the $(n,k)$ cells we test, the brute-force
oracle achieves in-sample Sharpe between $0.91$ and $1.16$; on the $20\%$ held-out
block of the same panel its out-of-sample Sharpe drops to $0.46$--$0.71$, a relative
collapse of $30$ to $50\%$. Greedy, simulated annealing, and \textsc{Slh}\ inherit
identical collapses, because they recover the same support as the oracle. A better
in-sample optimum, it turns out, does not buy out-of-sample performance.

The bottleneck is not the optimisation pipeline; it is a sample-size limitation in
$\hat\mu$ and $\hat\Sigma$. Phase~1 placed the residual variance proxy at roughly
$5\times$ the Gaussian benchmark (from $\psi_2/\nu_{\mathrm{Gauss}}\approx 2.2$); for
$T=2{,}009$ training days, the per-coordinate signal-to-noise ratio of $\hat\mu_i$ is
bounded by a constant of order one, and Phase~3a's threshold $a_\star(\rho)$ is
essentially a count of the standard deviations separating planted signal from bulk.
With realistic financial data that margin is small, which makes the in-sample top-$k$
informative about some signal, while most of that signal is sample noise specific to
the training block. The $30$ to $50\%$ Sharpe collapse is the discrete-portfolio
analogue of the bias-variance trade-off familiar from high-dimensional regression:
the in-sample optimum is overfit to the realisation of $(\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma)$.

\subsection{Out-of-sample Sharpe: quantifying the statistical bottleneck}
\label{sec:oos-sharpe}
 
The in-sample/out-of-sample dichotomy of~\S\ref{ssec:ogp-discussion} relies on a
quantitative generalisation claim that we instantiate here. For each $(n,k)$ cell,
with $5$ random asset draws per cell and $\gamma=10$, we compute the in-sample (IS)
annualised Sharpe of the brute-force oracle on the $T_{\mathrm{tr}}=2009$-day train
block; the out-of-sample (OOS) Sharpe of the same support held fixed on the
$T_{\mathrm{te}}=503$-day held-out block; and the OOS Sharpe of three alternatives:
the polished \textsc{Slh} output, the \textsc{Top-$k$-$\hat\mu$} support (top-$k$
stocks by $\hat\mu_i$, no covariance adjustment), and the equal-weight portfolio
across all $n$ stocks in the cell (\textsc{Eq-$n$}, no selection). All Sharpe ratios
are annualised by $\sqrt{252}$. Standard errors use the iid-Gaussian approximation
$\mathrm{SE}(\mathrm{Sh}) \approx \sqrt{(1+\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Sh}^2)/T_{\mathrm{te}}}$
of~\cite{lo-sharpe}, giving $\mathrm{SE}\approx 0.05$ at
$\mathrm{Sh}\in[0.2,0.8]$ on $T_{\mathrm{te}}=503$. That an equal-weight benchmark can
match or beat optimised Markowitz portfolios out-of-sample is the well-documented
$1/N$ phenomenon~\cite{demiguel-garlappi-uppal}; our table is a discrete-portfolio
restatement of it.

\paragraph{Reading the table.}
First, the oracle's relative Sharpe drop is $30$ to $53\%$ (pooled $43\%$), more than
$8\times$ a single Sharpe standard error; the brute-force optimum overfits
substantially, and the effect cannot be attributed to Monte-Carlo noise. This is the
central quantitative claim of~\S\ref{ssec:ogp-discussion}.

Second, \textsc{Slh} and the oracle have OOS Sharpe within one standard error on every
cell, and exactly equal on $4/5$ cells. On $(40,5)$, \textsc{Slh}'s seed lands on a
non-oracle support in $1/5$ instances, costing $0.07$ in OOS Sharpe (well within SE).
The in-sample claim of~\S\ref{sec:slh-empirical} (that \textsc{Slh} recovers the
oracle support) therefore carries through out-of-sample: the two methods are
statistically indistinguishable in held-out performance, and equal on the IS
objective by construction.

Third, the unselected equal-weight baseline \textsc{Eq-$n$} beats the oracle on $3/5$
cells, ties within SE on $(30,8)$, loses on $(50,5)$, and pools to $0.64$ vs $0.56$ --
the only strategy whose OOS Sharpe is not statistically below its IS Sharpe. This is
the strongest form of the ``statistically hard'' claim: the cardinality-constrained
optimum on $(\hat\mu, \hat\Sigma)$ is not merely worse out-of-sample than in-sample,
it is on average worse than a baseline that performs no selection at all. The
covariance penalty does buy something -- the oracle's pooled $0.56$ versus
\textsc{Top-$k$-$\hat\mu$}'s $0.40$ is a $0.16$ gap worth $\approx 3$ SEs -- but not
enough to clear the no-information baseline. This is the discrete-portfolio analogue
of the $1/N$ result of~\cite{demiguel-garlappi-uppal}.
 
\paragraph{Reconciling with the in-sample story.}
The OOS results complicate the practical narrative of~\S\ref{sec:slh-empirical}
without contradicting it. In-sample, \textsc{Slh} recovers the oracle's support at
$0.1$\,ms per call -- the in-sample optimisation is solved. Out-of-sample, the
recovered support is overfit to the realisation of $(\hat\mu, \hat\Sigma)$ in a
sample-size-limited regime ($T_{\mathrm{tr}}=2009$,
$\hat\nu^2 \approx 5\nu^2_{\mathrm{Gauss}}$). The bottleneck is not the discrete
optimisation, it is the quality of the moments fed into it. This is consistent with
the regime placement of~\S\ref{sec:slh-regime}: real S\&P daily returns sit in
Regime~I (Table~\ref{tab:regime-numbers}), where the planted-spike model is a poor
fit and the in-sample optimum is fitting a realisation of the noise $g$, not a
genuine signal $\sigma^\star$. Out-of-sample performance therefore collapses regardless
of which algorithm attains the in-sample optimum.
 
\paragraph{Implications.}
The follow-up question raised by Table~\ref{tab:oos-sharpe} is whether shrinkage
estimators of $(\hat\mu, \hat\Sigma)$ -- Ledoit-Wolf~\cite{ledoit-wolf} for the
covariance, James-Stein for the mean -- can close the gap between the
cardinality-constrained oracle and \textsc{Eq-$n$}. We leave this as the principal
practical follow-up. The regime framework of~\S\ref{sec:regimes} suggests one
possible mechanism: shrinkage that pulls $\hat\mu$ toward the cross-sectional mean
effectively rescales the planted-spike SNR, potentially moving the problem out of
Regime~I (where the planted model is too weak to bind) and into Regime~II
(``algorithmically hard but informative''), at which point the algorithmic content of
this paper would become substantively binding.

\paragraph{The role of the OGP theory in this story.} The Phase~3 theorems and the
hardness corollary are not directly responsible for the in-sample performance of
\textsc{Slh}\ on real data: as discussed in~\S\ref{sec:slh-regime}, the empirical SNR
sits below detection (Regime~I), where the OGP-active regime does not apply. The
theory does two other useful things. It quantifies the boundary above which any
algorithm could conceivably recover the planted optimum (\cref{thm:detect}). And it
identifies an explicit forbidden-overlap interval (\cref{thm:forbid}) for the linear
score, together with a conjectural classical OGP for the full quadratic objective
that the mixing-time blow-up of constrained Metropolis between $(20,5)$ and
$(40,10)$ in~\S\ref{ssec:p2b} supports. Any move to a regime with stronger per-asset
signal -- via shrinkage of $\hat\mu$, or different markets, or different sampling
frequency -- is the territory the algorithm would have to navigate.

\paragraph{Where the project would go next.} The most practically pressing direction
is statistical regularisation of $\hat\mu,\hat\Sigma$. A Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage
estimator~\cite{ledoit-wolf}, or a Bayesian factor-model prior, would attack the
out-of-sample collapse directly. Whether shrinkage moves the empirical SNR closer to
$a_\star(\rho)$ -- turning the problem from overfit but combinatorially easy into
honest but combinatorially harder -- is the concrete question the regime framework
of~\S\ref{sec:slh-regime} sets up.

On the theoretical side, the principal open question is a second-moment proof of the
classical OGP for $J$. The empirical mixing-time blow-up of~\S\ref{ssec:p2b} is
suggestive but not definitive; a Sherrington-Kirkpatrick-style second-moment
computation on the spike-plus-bulk model of~\eqref{eq:factor-noise} would convert the
conjecture into a theorem. The relevant machinery is the Gaussian-comparison and
Lindeberg toolkit cited in~\S\ref{sec:ogp}.

Finally, the experimental envelope is narrow. We fixed $\gamma=10$ throughout; a
sensitivity analysis at $\gamma\in\{1,10,100\}$ would test whether the
in-sample/out-of-sample story is robust to this choice. The Metropolis OGP evidence
in~\S\ref{ssec:p2b} could be strengthened with multiple computational budgets, full
trace plots, and a comparison of cold-start versus planted-start hitting times. We
report basic hitting-time statistics here and treat the thorough characterisation as
follow-up.
